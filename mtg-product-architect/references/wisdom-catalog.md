# Product Architecture Wisdom Catalog

The product architect's job is to ensure that a great set of cards becomes a great product line. This catalog encodes the failures, paradoxes, and hard-won insights that determine whether a Magic product succeeds commercially — because a brilliant set in the wrong product wrapper still fails.

---

## Product Failure Stories

### 1. The Overpowered Precon Problem

**True-Name Nemesis** (Commander 2013) — A card designed for Commander precons that was a three-mana uncounterable, unkillable threat in Legacy. It warped Legacy tournament play, drove the precon's price to $60+ (far above MSRP), and created a feel-bad experience where buying a casual product was required to play a competitive format. The lesson: precon-exclusive cards must be tested against ALL formats they're legal in, not just their intended home.

**Dockside Extortionist** (Commander 2019, Mystic Intellect deck) — A two-mana goblin that generates Treasure tokens equal to opponents' artifacts and enchantments. Rapidly escalated from precon inclusion to $35, then cracked $70+ on the secondary market before eventually being banned. Every stat was dialed to maximum. The lesson: cards that scale with the number of opponents are inherently dangerous because Commander always has three opponents.

**Fierce Guardianship** (Commander 2020, Ikoria) — Free Negate when you control your commander. Immediately became an auto-include in virtually every blue Commander deck. The precon containing it was the hardest to find in stores. The lesson: free spells are always dangerous, and a cycle of them multiplies the problem.

**Jeweled Lotus** (Commander Legends 2020) — Verhey publicly stated it was a mistake. Banned in September 2024. The danger was "ubiquity" — it pushed toward homogenized deckbuilding where every deck ran the same cards. WotC's current heuristic: avoid making cards that go in EVERY Commander deck.

**Arcane Signet** — Also regretted by Verhey as an auto-include that shouldn't have been created. When a card is correct in 100% of decks, it eliminates a deckbuilding decision and homogenizes the format.

### 2. The Underpowered Precon Problem

**Early Commander precons (2011-2012)** had mana bases so poor (mostly basics and taplands) that the decks barely functioned. Players bought them for 1-2 cards and threw the rest away. The lesson: a precon's mana base IS its play experience. Investing in a functional mana base (even if not optimal) is the difference between a product that creates new players and one that frustrates them.

**Planeswalker Decks (2017-2020)** — Designed to be simple entry products with exclusive planeswalkers that were intentionally underpowered. The problem: they were SO weak that experienced players wouldn't recommend them, and new players who bought them felt cheated when they lost every game at the LGS. Discontinued and replaced by Starter Kits. The lesson: entry products must be weak enough to not warp competitive play but strong enough that the buyer has FUN.

### 3. The Product Nobody Wanted

**Theme Boosters** — Introduced as a simpler alternative to Draft Boosters, each containing 35 cards in a single color. They offered terrible value (bulk commons/uncommons at premium pricing), no draft utility, and no chase potential. Players quickly identified them as a trap product. Discontinued when Play Boosters launched. The lesson: a product must have a clear value proposition for SOMEONE. Theme Boosters failed the question "who is this for?"

**Set Boosters** (2020-2023) — Introduced alongside Draft Boosters to create "an opening experience" with The List, art cards, and guaranteed foils. The problem: maintaining TWO booster products split the audience and confused retailers. Neither was clearly "the right one to buy." Eventually merged with Draft Boosters into Play Boosters. The lesson: product differentiation only works when the audience can self-sort. Two similar products with different names and different slot structures just creates confusion.

### 4. Product Fatigue

**2023 Release Overload** — WotC released an unprecedented volume of products: March of the Machine, Aftermath, Lord of the Rings, Wilds of Eldraine, Doctor Who Commander, Lost Caverns of Ixalan, plus Secret Lairs and supplemental products. In February 2023, a Bank of America report accused Hasbro of "killing its golden goose" by overproducing Magic to prop up short-term results, causing a 5.2% stock dip. March of the Machine: The Aftermath — a 50-card micro-set sold in 5-card Epilogue Booster packs — received an all-time Magic low of **5% player approval**. It was undraftable, leaked online before release, and killed all future Epilogue expansions. **Beyond Boosters** (Assassin's Creed, 2024) were also confirmed as a failure by Rosewater — being undraftable drastically reduced purchase volume. Rosewater acknowledged feedback and WotC reduced from 9 main products in 2024 to 7 in 2025 and cut Commander precons from 24 to 13. The lesson: every product competes for the same wallet. Adding more products without adding more value just spreads spending thinner and burns goodwill.

**Universes Beyond Saturation** — As UB products increased (Warhammer, LotR, Doctor Who, Fallout, Final Fantasy, Marvel, Spider-Man), some players expressed fatigue with non-Magic IPs. The most successful UB products (LotR, Final Fantasy) succeeded because the IP resonated deeply with Magic's existing audience. Less resonant IPs risked feeling like licensed products using Magic's rules rather than Magic products. The lesson: IP selection matters more than IP volume. One deeply resonant IP beats three shallowly connected ones.

## The Commander Precon Paradox

Commander precons must navigate four simultaneous contradictions:

### The Power Paradox
- **Too weak** → Nobody buys them (Planeswalker Decks syndrome)
- **Too strong** → They warp the format and create must-buy obligations (True-Name Nemesis syndrome)
- **Sweet spot**: Power level 6-7 on the casual 1-10 scale. Strong enough to win games at a casual table, weak enough that upgrading is both possible and rewarding

### The Connection Paradox
- **Too connected** → Precon requires main set cards to function, making it an incomplete product that demands additional purchases
- **Too disconnected** → Precon feels like it belongs to a different set, missing the thematic resonance that drives impulse purchases
- **Sweet spot**: Precon themes use the same MECHANICAL SPACE as the main set (same keywords, same synergy axes) but don't need specific main set cards. Players who draft the main set will naturally find cards that slot into their precon

### The Reprint Paradox
- **Too many chase reprints** → The precon becomes a reprint vehicle, undercutting the secondary market and making players wait for precons instead of buying boosters
- **Too few valuable reprints** → The precon feels like it's full of bulk, poor perceived value
- **Sweet spot**: 3-5 meaningful reprints ($3-10 range) that serve the deck's strategy, plus 1-2 slightly higher-value reprints that reward purchasers

### The New Card Paradox
- **Too many pushed new cards** → Creates format staples that are only available through precons (Dockside Extortionist pattern)
- **Too few exciting new cards** → No reason to buy the precon over building the deck yourself
- **Sweet spot**: 10-15 new cards per precon. The commander and 2-3 signature cards are splashy and exciting. The rest are solid role-players that support the theme but aren't format-defining

## Chase Card Psychology

What makes a card a "chase card" that drives booster sales:

### The Chase Card Hierarchy
1. **Competitive staple** — Cards that define tournament decks. These drive competitive players to crack packs. Examples: Sheoldred, the Apocalypse; The One Ring; Omnath, Locus of Creation
2. **Commander staple** — Cards every Commander deck wants. These drive the largest audience. Examples: Smothering Tithe, Rhystic Study, Cyclonic Rift
3. **Iconic character** — Beloved characters that fans collect regardless of power. Examples: any Jace planeswalker, The One Ring (character AND power), Gandalf
4. **Beautiful art** — Cards sought for their art, especially in premium treatments. Examples: showcase or borderless versions of popular cards
5. **Build-around mythic** — Cards that spawn new deck archetypes. Players buy packs hoping to open the card that lets them build something new

### The Poster Card Principle
Every set needs 3-5 "poster cards" — the cards that appear in previews, that players discuss before release, that drive pre-orders. These cards must be:
- **Visible** — Previewed early and prominently
- **Exciting** — Either powerful, unique, flavorful, or all three
- **Diverse** — Spread across colors so every player sees something for them
- **Available** — At mythic/rare in regular boosters, not locked behind premium-only products

## The Special Treatment Evolution

Each special treatment was introduced to solve a specific problem:

| Treatment | Introduced | Problem Solved | Verdict |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Showcase frames** | Throne of Eldraine (2019) | Sets lacked visual personality beyond art | Success — each set now has a unique frame identity |
| **Borderless art** | War of the Spark (2019) | Premium versions felt underwhelming | Success — borderless planeswalkers/mythics are consistently popular |
| **Extended art** | Throne of Eldraine (2019) | Collector Boosters needed exclusive content | Moderate — less exciting than borderless but fills the role |
| **Full-art lands** | Various (ongoing) | Basic lands were boring | Perennial success — always popular |
| **Serialized cards** | Brothers' War (2022) | Collector Boosters needed ultra-premium chase | Polarizing — exciting for some, predatory for others |
| **Retro frames** | Time Spiral Remastered (2021) | Nostalgia monetization | Success — old-border reprints have strong collector appeal |
| **Textured foil** | Double Masters 2022 | Regular foils lost their premium feel (too common) | Success — restored the "special" feeling to premium cards |
| **Confetti foil / Surge foil** | Various | Foil differentiation | Mixed — too many foil variants created confusion |

### The Treatment Allocation Rule
- **Mythics**: Borderless art, showcase frames, textured foils — maximum treatment variety
- **Rares**: Showcase frames, extended art — moderate treatment
- **Uncommons**: Showcase frames only (selected subset)
- **Commons**: Full-art basic lands only

## Named Heuristics

### 1. The Poster Card Test
Can you point to 3-5 specific cards that will make players say "I want to open that"? If you can't identify the poster cards, the set lacks commercial hooks. These should be spread across at least 3 colors and include at least one legendary creature for Commander.

### 2. The Precon Paradox Test
For each Commander precon: Would a player be happy playing this straight out of the box against other precons? AND would a player who builds decks from scratch consider buying specific cards from this precon? If either answer is no, the precon is mispositioned.

### 3. The Collector Hook Test
Does the Collector Booster contain at least one type of content that CANNOT be found in Play Boosters? Exclusive treatments (textured foils, serialized cards, special showcase frames) justify the premium price. If a Collector Booster is just "more foils," it fails the hook test.

### 4. The Theme Connection Test
Can a player who bought a precon walk into an LGS, draft the main set, and find cards that naturally slot into their precon deck? The connection should be mechanical (shared keywords, shared synergy axes), not just thematic. A "graveyard matters" precon complements a set with graveyard mechanics even if the flavors are different.

### 5. The Product Fatigue Check
For each product in the suite, ask: "If we didn't make this, would anyone notice?" Every product must serve a specific audience with a specific value proposition. If two products serve the same audience with similar value, combine them. Aftermath taught us that micro-sets nobody asked for burn more goodwill than they generate revenue.

### 6. The Chase Distribution Test
Map every card worth $5+ across products. If all the chase is concentrated in Collector Boosters, Play Boosters feel empty. If all the chase is in precons, booster sales suffer. Chase should be distributed: poster mythics in Play Boosters, precon-exclusive commanders in precons, premium treatments in Collector Boosters.

### 7. The New Player Ramp Test
Can a new player enter Magic through ANY product in the suite and have a good experience? Starter Kits → precons → Play Boosters should form a progression. If a product requires format knowledge to evaluate (Collector Boosters), it shouldn't be marketed at new players.

### 8. The Whale Value Test
For the most expensive product (Collector Booster box), does the expected value justify the price for someone who buys everything? Whales are real customers with real expectations. If a Collector Booster box averages $80 in card value for a $250 purchase, repeat customers will stop buying.
