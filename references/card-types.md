# Card Types

This is a comprehensive catalog of every card type and mechanically significant subtype available for set design. Its purpose is to prevent the skill from defaulting to only the most common types (Creature, Instant, Sorcery, Enchantment, Artifact, Land) and missing types that could serve the set's themes better.

**During Phase 5 (Card File), consult this catalog.** Before filling each skeleton slot, ask: "Is a creature really the best fit here, or would a Saga, Battle, Vehicle, or Equipment better express what this card is trying to do?" The skeleton provides mana values and roles — this catalog provides the full menu of *how* to fill those roles.

## Card Types (permanents)

These stay on the battlefield after resolving.

### Creature

The most common card type. Creatures attack, block, and carry keywords and abilities. Every color gets creatures; most commons are creatures.

**When to use:** Whenever the card represents a being that fights or has an ongoing presence.

**Creature type (subtype) notes:** Creature subtypes are the tribal identity of the set — Human, Elf, Goblin, Merfolk, Phyrexian, Dinosaur, etc. Choose creature types that fit the world (Phase 2). A set typically uses 15–25 creature types, with 3–5 being mechanically relevant (tribal payoffs). Avoid creating new creature types when existing ones fit — the game has over 280 creature types and players connect with familiar ones.

### Artifact

Colorless (usually) permanents representing objects, devices, and constructions. Artifacts can be creatures too (Artifact Creature). Their colorlessness means any deck can play them, which makes them powerful glue cards in limited.

**When to use:** Objects, tools, machines, magical items, constructions. Also useful as colorless support cards that any archetype can use, and as mana-fixing (mana rocks).

**Key subtypes:**

- **Equipment** — artifacts that attach to creatures, granting abilities or stat boosts. Equipment persists when the creature dies, unlike Auras. Great for sets with warrior/soldier themes, adventure themes, or any world where gear matters. A set typically has 2–5 Equipment cards.

- **Vehicle** — artifacts with power and toughness that only become creatures when "crewed" (tapping creatures with total power meeting a threshold). Vehicles represent ships, chariots, airships, mounts-as-objects, siege engines, etc. They're excellent for sets with travel, war, racing, or exploration themes. Introduced in Kaladesh (2016). A set with a vehicle theme might have 5–10; a set without might have 0–2.

- **Treasure** — artifact tokens that sacrifice for one mana of any color. A common design tool for mana-fixing and ramp. Many cards create Treasure tokens rather than being Treasure cards themselves.

- **Food** — artifact tokens that sacrifice (pay 2 and tap) to gain 3 life. Strong flavor fit for sets with feast, harvest, survival, or comfort themes. Introduced in Throne of Eldraine (2019).

- **Clue** — artifact tokens that sacrifice (pay 2) to draw a card. Perfect for mystery, investigation, detective, or knowledge-seeking themes. Introduced in Shadows over Innistrad (2016), featured heavily in Murders at Karlov Manor.

- **Blood** — artifact tokens that can be discarded with a card to draw a card (looting). Fits horror, vampire, or sacrifice themes. Introduced in Innistrad: Crimson Vow (2021).

- **Map** — artifact tokens that let you explore (look at top card, keep or put in graveyard with +1/+1 counter on a creature). Fits exploration and adventure themes. Introduced in The Lost Caverns of Ixalan (2023).

- **Powerstone** — artifact tokens that tap for colorless mana (can't be spent on nonartifact spells). Fits artifact-heavy or industrial themes. Introduced in The Brothers' War (2022).

- **Incubator** — artifact tokens that transform into Phyrexian creature tokens. Specific to Phyrexian themes. Introduced in March of the Machine (2023).

- **Fortification** — artifacts that attach to lands (the land equivalent of Equipment). Extremely rare — only one has ever been printed (Darksteel Garrison). Could be explored in a set where land-upgrading is a theme.

### Enchantment

Permanents representing ongoing magical effects, laws, curses, blessings, and ambient conditions. Enchantments tend to be more fragile than artifacts (fewer removal options hit them in some colors) but are excellent for representing persistent world states.

**When to use:** Ongoing effects, rules changes, passive bonuses, curses, blessings, ambient magic, environmental conditions, narrative progression.

**Key subtypes:**

- **Aura** — enchantments that attach to a permanent (usually a creature) or player. Auras that buff creatures are a staple of every set. The risk: if the creature dies, the Aura goes too (unlike Equipment). Every set uses Auras; typically 5–12 across the set.

- **Saga** — enchantments that tell a story over 2–4 chapters (turns). Each chapter triggers an effect, then the Saga sacrifices itself after the final chapter. Sagas are *exceptional* for sets based on existing IPs or rich lore — they let you mechanically recreate iconic story moments. Introduced in Dominaria (2018). A lore-heavy set might have 5–10 Sagas; a mechanics-first set might have 0–3.

- **Class** — enchantments that "level up" by paying mana costs, unlocking progressively stronger abilities at each tier (Level 1, 2, 3). Perfect for RPG themes, character growth, skill progression, or education themes. Introduced in Adventures in the Forgotten Realms (2021).

- **Case** — enchantments that start with one effect and "solve" when a condition is met, unlocking a second effect. Great for mystery, investigation, or puzzle themes. Introduced in Murders at Karlov Manor (2024).

- **Room** — split enchantments where each half can be cast separately, and the second half can be "unlocked" later. Represents physical spaces. Perfect for haunted house, dungeon, exploration, or architecture themes. Introduced in Duskmourn: House of Horror (2024).

- **Curse** — Auras that enchant *players* (not permanents), imposing negative effects. Excellent for horror, dark magic, or vengeance themes. Introduced in Innistrad (2011).

- **Shrine** — legendary enchantments that scale with the number of Shrines you control. Fit religious, temple, or devotion themes. Introduced in Champions of Kamigawa (2004), revisited in Core Set 2021.

- **Role** — Aura tokens assigned to creatures representing narrative roles (Wicked, Royal, Young Hero, etc.). Only one Role per creature per player. Fit fairy tale or narrative themes. Introduced in Wilds of Eldraine (2023).

- **Rune** — Auras that can enchant any permanent and draw a card on entry. Fit Nordic, magical inscription, or crafting themes. Introduced in Kaldheim (2021).

- **Background** — legendary enchantments that interact with Commanders. Primarily a Commander product mechanic. Introduced in Commander Legends: Battle for Baldur's Gate (2022).

- **Cartouche** — Auras that give +1/+1 and a keyword, representing achievements or trials. Fit Egyptian or trial/achievement themes. Introduced in Amonkhet (2017).

- **Shard** — predefined enchantment token type. Rarely used.

### Planeswalker

Permanents representing powerful allied characters. They enter with loyalty counters and activate one ability per turn (plus or minus loyalty). Opponents can attack them.

**When to use:** Major named characters, powerful allies, story-central figures. Planeswalkers are rare or mythic only. A standard set has 3–5 planeswalkers. As of recent design philosophy, WotC has been moving planeswalkers more toward mythic-only and making them less frequent.

**Design consideration:** Planeswalkers are the most complex card type. They create subgames about protecting/attacking them. Use them for characters the set *needs* to showcase, not as filler for the mythic slot.

### Battle

The newest permanent card type, introduced in March of the Machine (2023). Battles enter the battlefield with defense counters. Opponents (or the caster, depending on subtype) can attack them to remove counters. When defeated, they exile and return transformed.

**Subtype: Siege** — the only Battle subtype so far. When a Siege enters, its controller chooses an opponent as its protector. Everyone except the protector can attack it. When defeated, it transforms into its back face (usually a powerful permanent) under its owner's control.

**When to use:** Battles are excellent for sets with themes of conflict, siege warfare, territorial conquest, ideological struggle, invasion, or any scenario where the narrative involves *overcoming an obstacle over time*. They represent something being fought over — a location, an ideal, a resource. The two-sided nature (front = the battle, back = the reward for winning) makes them naturally dramatic.

**Design consideration:** Battles are still new and WotC is exploring the design space. A custom set could use them with the Siege subtype or potentially define a new Battle subtype if the set's theme warrants it (e.g., "Quest" battles that the caster attacks, "Ritual" battles that tick down automatically). A set might have 0 (if battles don't fit the theme) to 10+ (if conflict is a central pillar).

**Thematic fit examples:**
- War/invasion set → Siege battles representing contested locations
- Quest/adventure set → Battles representing challenges to overcome
- Political intrigue set → Battles representing power struggles
- Horror set → Battles representing cursed locations that must be cleansed
- My Little Pony set → Battles representing villain threats that must be defeated through friendship

### Land

Permanents that produce mana. One land per turn (usually). Lands are the resource system of Magic.

**When to use:** Mana production, fixing, utility effects. Every set has basic lands; most sets also have 5–15 nonbasic lands at uncommon through rare.

**Key subtypes:** Plains, Island, Swamp, Mountain, Forest (the five basic land types — cards that reference these types interact with dual lands that have them). Other subtypes: Desert, Lair, Locus, Mine, Power-Plant, Tower, Urza's, Gate, Sphere.

**Design consideration for custom sets:** Utility lands (lands with activated abilities beyond mana production) are a great way to add depth without taking spell slots. Dual lands at uncommon or rare provide mana-fixing for the draft format. A "lands-matter" set might have significantly more nonbasic lands than usual.

### Kindred (formerly Tribal)

A type that can appear on non-creature spells to give them creature subtypes. A "Kindred Instant — Elf" is an instant that counts as an Elf for tribal synergies. Renamed from "Tribal" in 2024.

**When to use:** When the set has strong tribal themes and you want non-creature spells to interact with tribal payoffs. A tribal-heavy set might use Kindred on 5–15 cards; most sets use 0.

**Design consideration:** Kindred exists specifically to let instants, sorceries, and enchantments carry creature types. If your set has cards that count Elves, a Kindred Sorcery — Elf removal spell counts as an Elf for those payoffs.

## Card Types (non-permanents)

These go to the graveyard after resolving (unless exiled or otherwise redirected).

### Instant

Spells that can be cast at any time you have priority, including on opponents' turns and in response to other spells.

**When to use:** Reactive effects (counterspells, combat tricks, removal in response to threats), surprise effects, and anything where timing flexibility is the point.

**Key subtypes:**
- **Adventure** — a split card where one half is an Adventure (instant or sorcery) and the other is a creature. Cast the Adventure first, exile it, then cast the creature later. Fits fairy tale, quest, or journey themes. Introduced in Throne of Eldraine (2019).
- **Trap** — instants with an alternate (cheaper) cost if a condition is met. Fits ambush, defense, or cunning themes. Introduced in Zendikar (2009).
- **Lesson** — spells that can be fetched from outside the game by the "learn" mechanic. Fits school or education themes. Introduced in Strixhaven (2021).
- **Arcane** — a parasitic subtype from original Kamigawa for "Splice onto Arcane." Generally avoided as a cautionary example of parasitism.

### Sorcery

Spells that can only be cast during your main phase when the stack is empty. Sorceries tend to have more powerful effects than instants to compensate for the timing restriction.

**When to use:** Board wipes, tutors, large-scale effects, summoning rituals, anything where the restriction to your own turn is acceptable. Sorceries share all the instant subtypes (Adventure, Trap, Lesson, Arcane).

## Supertypes

Supertypes modify any card type and add rules meaning:

- **Basic** — only on lands. Basic lands can have any number in a deck (unlike the 4-copy limit). The five basic land types (Plains, Island, Swamp, Mountain, Forest) have intrinsic mana abilities.

- **Legendary** — the legend rule: you can only control one legendary permanent with a given name at a time. Used for named characters, unique locations, iconic artifacts. Legendary creatures can be Commanders. Sets typically have 15–30 legendary cards (mostly at rare/mythic).

- **Snow** — permanents and spells that interact with "snow mana" (mana produced by snow permanents). Fits winter, ice, frozen, or Nordic themes. A snow set replaces basic lands with Snow-Covered basics.

- **World** — extremely old supertype (only on enchantments). The "world rule" means only one World enchantment can exist at a time. Essentially obsolete but technically still in the rules.

## Using this catalog during design

When filling skeleton slots in Phase 5, use this checklist:

1. **Review the set's themes and pillars.** Which types and subtypes naturally fit? A horror set should consider Curses, Sagas (for backstory), and possibly Rooms. A war set should consider Battles and Vehicles. A mystery set should consider Cases and Clues.

2. **Don't default to creatures for every slot.** The skeleton marks many slots as "creature," but some of those can be replaced with other permanent types that fill the same role more flavorfully. A 4-mana "defender" slot could be a Vehicle that needs crewing, a Battle that blocks attacks by being an alternative target, or an enchantment that creates tokens.

3. **Check for underused types.** If your set file has zero Sagas, zero Equipment, zero Vehicles, and zero Battles, ask whether that's a deliberate choice or an oversight. Most modern sets use at least 2–3 of these subtypes.

4. **Match type to narrative role.** What is the card *trying to say*?
   - "This character has a signature weapon" → Equipment
   - "This is a story from the world's history" → Saga
   - "This is a challenge to overcome" → Battle
   - "This is an ongoing curse or blessing" → Enchantment (possibly Curse or Aura)
   - "This is a vehicle or mount" → Vehicle
   - "This place has power" → Legendary Land
   - "This investigation yielded evidence" → creates Clue tokens
   - "This is a feast or harvest" → creates Food tokens
   - "This character grows over time" → Class or leveler creature

5. **Note type-specific balance considerations.** Equipment and Vehicles are colorless, so they can go in any deck — be careful about making them too strong. Battles add complexity to the board state, so limit them if the set is already complex. Sagas take multiple turns to resolve, affecting format speed.
