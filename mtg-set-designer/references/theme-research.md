# Theme Research

Phase 0.5 exists because the most common failure mode for top-down sets isn't bad mechanics — it's shallow understanding of the source material. A designer who *thinks* they know an IP well enough to skip research will produce a set full of surface-level references and miss the structural elements that would have made the set feel deeply authentic.

This phase produces a **Theme Brief** — a structured analysis of the source material that the Vision phase (Phase 1) can build pillars from with confidence. The Theme Brief lives in `design_doc.md` between the Intake section and the Vision section.

## When to do deep research

Always. Even for themes the model knows well. The depth varies:

- **Licensed IP** (My Little Pony, Lord of the Rings, Warhammer): Full research. The audience knows this material intimately and will notice every gap.
- **Historical/cultural setting** (feudal Japan, Norse mythology, Aztec empire): Full research, plus a cultural sensitivity pass.
- **Genre** (gothic horror, space opera, heist): Moderate research. Identify the genre's conventions, tropes, and subversions so the set can lean into or against them deliberately.
- **Original concept** (a world where gravity reverses, a plane made of music): Light research into real-world analogues and adjacent fiction that has explored similar ideas. You're not adapting a source — you're building one — but knowing what's been done prevents accidental cliché.
- **Mechanical concept** (lands-matter, graveyard-as-resource): Light thematic research. The mechanical idea is the driver, but you still need a world to dress it in, and that world should be informed by research into settings that would naturally produce those mechanics.

## Step 1 — User interview

Before researching, ask the user targeted questions. The goal is to understand their *intent*, not to extract a design brief from them. Keep it to 3–5 questions max.

**Always ask:**

- **Which version / era / interpretation?** IPs evolve. My Little Pony has four generations with radically different tones. Lord of the Rings means different things depending on whether you mean the books, the Peter Jackson films, or the Rings of Power series. Aztec mythology means different things depending on whether the user wants the cosmology, the political structure, or the military culture.
- **Faithful adaptation or creative reinterpretation?** A faithful MLP set keeps the candy-colored optimism. A reinterpretation might explore what Equestria looks like during a war. Both are valid — they produce completely different sets.
- **What's the emotional hook?** Ask the user *why* they want this theme. The answer reveals what the set needs to deliver. "I want MLP because my daughter loves it" produces a different set than "I want MLP because the friendship-vs-villainy dynamic is mechanically rich."

**Ask if relevant:**

- **Audience familiarity** — are the players expected to know the source material, or should the set introduce it? This affects how much the cards can rely on resonance vs. how much they need to be self-explanatory.
- **Tone boundaries** — are there aspects of the theme the user wants to avoid? (Violence level, specific characters, controversial storylines.)
- **Crossover awareness** — does the user know about existing MTG crossovers with this IP (Universes Beyond, Secret Lair)? Do they want to align with or diverge from those?

## Step 2 — Web research

Use web search to build a factual foundation. You are not trying to become an expert — you are trying to avoid being *wrong* about the basics and to discover structural elements you didn't know existed.

**Search strategy:**

1. **Overview search** — "[theme] overview" or "[IP name] summary" to get the broad strokes: setting, main characters, central conflict, tone.
2. **Structure search** — "[IP] factions" or "[IP] groups" or "[theme] categories" to find natural groupings that could map to MTG's color pairs or tribes.
3. **Iconic elements search** — "[IP] most iconic moments" or "[IP] most famous [characters/locations/items]" to build the resonance inventory.
4. **Fan community search** — "[IP] fan wiki" or "[IP] TV Tropes" to find the community's understanding of the property's structure. Fan wikis are goldmines for faction breakdowns, character relationships, and thematic analysis.
5. **Existing MTG crossover search** — "MTG [IP] Universes Beyond" or "MTG [theme] Secret Lair" to find what WotC has already done.
6. **Critical analysis search** — "[IP] themes analysis" or "[IP] what makes it work" to find deeper structural analysis, often from academic or long-form fan writing.

For cultural/historical themes, add:

7. **Scholarly search** — "[culture] mythology structure" or "[historical period] social structure" to find expert-level overviews.
8. **Sensitivity search** — "[culture] representation in games" or "[culture] cultural appropriation concerns" to identify landmines.

**What to extract from research:**

- **Factions / groups / tribes** — every IP has natural groupings. MLP has earth ponies, pegasi, unicorns, alicorns. Lord of the Rings has the Free Peoples, the forces of Mordor, the Istari, the Ents. These are your archetype and color-pair candidates.
- **Central conflicts** — what drives the story? Friendship vs. chaos (MLP), free will vs. fate (Greek mythology), survival vs. greed (Zendikar-style adventure). These become your set's dramatic engine.
- **Iconic characters** — the characters players will expect to see as legendary creatures or planeswalkers. Note their abilities, personality, and relationships.
- **Iconic locations** — settings that could become legendary lands or that anchor cycles.
- **Iconic objects** — artifacts, weapons, tools that could become equipment or artifact cards.
- **Iconic moments** — scenes or events the audience would recognize and want to see referenced on cards (flavor text, card names, or mechanical callbacks).
- **Emotional register** — is the source material whimsical, grim, epic, intimate, comedic, tragic? This directly sets the tone of the play experience.
- **Power structure** — how is power distributed in this world? Who has it, who doesn't, who's trying to get it? This maps naturally onto MTG's color philosophy.
- **Magic system (if any)** — how does magic work in the source? This often suggests mechanics. MLP's cutie marks (innate destiny-linked abilities) suggest a very different mechanic than Lord of the Rings' ring-based corruption.

## Step 3 — Source material decomposition

Organize your research into a structured breakdown. This is the analytical step where raw facts become design inputs.

### Faction mapping

List every natural grouping in the source material and tentatively map it to one or two MTG colors based on philosophy, not aesthetics. The color pie is about *values*, not *visuals*:

- **White** — community, order, law, protection, religion, military structure
- **Blue** — knowledge, control, perfection, technology, manipulation, patience
- **Black** — ambition, self-interest, death, power at any cost, pragmatism
- **Red** — emotion, freedom, chaos, impulse, art, destruction, passion
- **Green** — nature, growth, tradition, instinct, acceptance, strength through size

Example for My Little Pony (Friendship is Magic):

| Faction | Colors | Reasoning |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Earth ponies | G/W | Community, agriculture, physical strength, tradition |
| Pegasi | U/R | Freedom, speed, weather control, emotion (Rainbow Dash) |
| Unicorns | U/W | Knowledge, magic mastery, elegance, structure (Canterlot) |
| Alicorns | W/U/variable | Rulers, balance, wisdom, protection |
| Chaos agents (Discord) | R/B or 5-color | Entropy, self-interest disguised as freedom |
| Changelings | B/U | Deception, infiltration, hunger, transformation |

This mapping is tentative. It will shift during Phase 4 (Archetype Grid) when you need exactly 10 two-color pairs. But having it here gives the Vision phase something to work with.

### Character roster (for character-rich IPs)

If the source material has a substantial cast of named characters, build a **character roster** before anything else in the resonance inventory. Character-rich IPs (My Little Pony, Lord of the Rings, Marvel, Star Wars, Avatar: The Last Airbender, etc.) succeed or fail based on whether fans see their favorite characters on cards. A set based on Friendship is Magic that doesn't have Twilight Sparkle, Rainbow Dash, and Fluttershy as individual cards has failed before it starts.

**Assess character density.** Ask: does this IP have 10+ named characters that fans would expect to see? If yes, the set is "character-rich" and the roster drives card design. If the IP has only a handful of characters (or is a genre/concept rather than a specific story), skip the roster and rely on the standard resonance inventory below.

**Build the roster as a table:**

| Character | Colors | Rarity | Card Type | Role in Set |
|-----------|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Twilight Sparkle | U/W | Mythic | Legendary Creature or Planeswalker | Main protagonist, card draw / knowledge theme |
| Rainbow Dash | U/R | Rare | Legendary Creature | Speed, flying, loyalty |
| Fluttershy | G/W | Rare | Legendary Creature | Creature buffing, pacifism |
| Discord | 5-color or R/B | Mythic | Legendary Creature | Chaos, randomness, transformation |
| Chrysalis | B/U | Rare | Legendary Creature | Shapeshifting, deception |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

For each character, note:

- **Color identity** — based on the character's *values and methods*, not their visual appearance. Use the color pie mapping from the faction analysis.
- **Rarity** — protagonists and main villains → mythic or rare. Major supporting cast → rare or uncommon. Minor but recognizable characters → uncommon. Background characters with fan followings → common (as non-legendary versions or referenced in flavor text).
- **Card type** — most characters become Legendary Creatures. The 1–3 most central characters might be Planeswalkers. Some characters are better expressed as other types: a character known for a single dramatic act might be a Saga; a character who IS a location might be a Legendary Land; a character's signature item might be a Legendary Artifact (Equipment).
- **Mechanical hook** — what makes this character feel *right* mechanically? Twilight Sparkle should interact with knowledge/spells/drawing. Rainbow Dash should be fast and evasive. Discord should be unpredictable. A character whose card doesn't feel like them is worse than no card at all.

**Character-to-slot targets for character-rich IPs:**

- **Mythic (20 slots):** 4–8 should be named characters (protagonists, main villains, iconic figures)
- **Rare (60 slots):** 10–20 should be named characters (major supporting cast, secondary villains, fan favorites)
- **Uncommon (100 slots):** 5–15 should be named characters (minor but recognizable cast members, especially as signpost uncommons when a character naturally fits an archetype)
- **Common (81 slots):** Named characters generally don't appear at common (commons represent the *masses* of the world, not individuals). However, common cards should reference named characters in flavor text, card names ("Twilight's Study," "Fluttershy's Songbird"), and art descriptions. This makes the characters feel present throughout the set even at common.

In total, a character-rich IP set should have **20–40 cards that are specific named characters**, plus another 30–50 cards that reference characters indirectly through names, flavor text, and art.

**Characters the set CAN'T fit:** If the IP has more named characters than the set can hold (Marvel, Star Wars, large anime casts), explicitly list the characters you're cutting and why. Prioritize: (1) characters central to the story/era being adapted, (2) characters fans most closely identify with the IP, (3) characters with distinct mechanical identities. Characters who are narratively important but mechanically redundant with another character should be cut or combined.

### Resonance inventory

List the elements that players who know the source material would be **disappointed not to see**. These are non-negotiable design targets — you don't have to make each one a card, but you have to acknowledge each one somewhere in the set.

Organize by type:

- **Must-have characters** — covered by the character roster above for character-rich IPs. For other sets, list the protagonists, main villain(s), and fan favorites here.
- **Must-have locations** — the iconic settings
- **Must-have objects** — the signature items
- **Must-have moments** — the scenes that define the IP (often become Sagas)
- **Must-have concepts** — the themes or mechanics unique to this world (cutie marks, the Elements of Harmony, friendship lessons)

### Anti-resonance inventory

List elements of the source material that **should not be cards** or that would translate poorly to MTG:

- **Elements that don't produce gameplay.** A friendship lesson is a great narrative beat but a card that says "learn a lesson about sharing" doesn't do anything in a game.
- **Elements that violate MTG's tone floor.** Even a lighthearted set needs creatures that can attack and block. If the source material has no conflict, you'll need to *find* or *invent* conflict.
- **Elements that are too obscure.** Deep-cut references that only 1% of the audience will recognize shouldn't take up common or uncommon slots. Save them for rare flavor text.
- **Elements that are mechanically identical to each other.** If the source has 15 characters who all do the same thing narratively, they can't all be unique cards.

### Tone calibration

Write a short paragraph (3–5 sentences) describing the set's intended tone, referencing the user's preferences from Step 1. Address:

- **Violence level** — MTG requires combat. How does this coexist with the source material's tone? A MLP set probably uses "banishment" and "containment" rather than "destruction" and "death."
- **Humor vs. gravity** — the Un-sets prove MTG can be funny. Is this set comedic, earnest, or serious?
- **Nostalgia vs. novelty** — is the set a love letter to existing fans or an attempt to introduce the IP through MTG's lens?

## Step 4 — Existing MTG crossover check

Search for and review any existing MTG products that touch this theme:

- **Universes Beyond** releases (full sets or commander decks)
- **Secret Lair** drops
- **Un-set** cards (Ponies: The Galloping exists for MLP specifically)
- **Thematically adjacent sets** (a Greek mythology theme should study Theros; a horror theme should study Innistrad)

For each, note:

- What mechanics did they use? Would you reuse or avoid them?
- What resonance points did they hit? What did they miss?
- What was the community reception? (Search "[product] review" or "[product] reddit" for community sentiment.)

This isn't about copying — it's about not reinventing what worked and not repeating what failed.

## Step 5 — Cultural sensitivity scan

For themes rooted in real-world cultures, religions, or historical events:

- **Identify sacred or sensitive elements** that should not be trivialized or gamified (religious figures, genocide, ongoing political conflicts).
- **Check for stereotyping risks** — are you reducing a complex culture to a few visual tropes?
- **Look for community guidance** — search "[culture] representation in games" to see what the affected community has said about how they want to be portrayed.
- **Note terminology carefully** — use the culture's own terms where appropriate, not anglicized or simplified versions.
- **Consider consultation** — note in the design doc if certain elements would benefit from cultural review by someone with lived experience.

This step may result in a "Sensitivity Notes" section in the design doc that flags specific cards or concepts for extra care during Phase 8 (Polish).

For fictional IPs, this step is lighter but not absent — even fictional properties have fan communities with strong feelings about how their beloved characters are portrayed.

## The Theme Brief — output format

The Theme Brief is a section in `design_doc.md` that appears after Intake and before Vision. Structure:

```markdown
## Theme Brief

### Source Material
[1–2 paragraphs summarizing the theme/IP, which version/era is being adapted, and the user's stated intent]

### Faction Mapping (tentative)
[Table mapping source factions to MTG colors with reasoning]

### Character Roster (if character-rich IP)
[Table: Character | Colors | Rarity | Card Type | Mechanical Hook]
[List characters cut and why, if the IP has more characters than the set can hold]

### Resonance Inventory
- **Characters:** [covered by roster above, or list here for non-character-rich sets]
- **Locations:** [list]
- **Objects:** [list]
- **Moments:** [list — note which would make good Sagas]
- **Concepts:** [list]

### Anti-Resonance Notes
[Bullet list of elements that won't translate or should be avoided, with reasons]

### Tone
[3–5 sentence tone statement]

### Existing MTG Crossovers
[Summary of what WotC has already done with this or adjacent themes]

### Sensitivity Notes (if applicable)
[Flags for cultural sensitivity concerns]

### Research Sources
[Links consulted during theme research — these go here, NOT in references/sources.md]
```

**Important:** Theme research links belong in the set's own `design_doc.md` under "Research Sources" — they are specific to this set. The skill-level `references/sources.md` is reserved for links used to build the skill itself (design theory articles, Rosewater columns, booster structure references, etc.) and should not be polluted with per-set research.

The Theme Brief is a *living document* during the design process. As later phases reveal that certain faction mappings don't produce good archetypes, or that a resonance point can't be mechanically expressed, update the brief rather than silently deviating from it.
