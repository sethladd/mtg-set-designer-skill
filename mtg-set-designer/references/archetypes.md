# The Ten Archetypes

Since original Ravnica (2005), standard Magic sets are built around a grid of **ten two-color draft archetypes**, one for each pair of colors. This is the scaffolding that makes draft work. Get this grid right and most of your card-level decisions become easy. Get it wrong and the format is broken before a single game is played.

## Why ten

Five colors, ten unordered pairs: WU, UB, BR, RG, GW (allied), WB, BG, GU, UR, RW (enemy). Each drafter at an 8-player table ends up in some color combination, and almost all of them land on a two-color deck. If every pair has a working archetype, eight drafters can find eight non-overlapping decks (or close to it). If one pair is missing, multiple drafters converge on the pairs that *do* work, the other archetypes get under-drafted, and the format becomes predictable and miserable.

Not every set uses exactly this grid — Shards of Alara built three-color wedges, Khans of Tarkir did five — but 10 two-color is the default and the right starting point unless your set's vision pillars demand otherwise.

## Archetype anatomy

An archetype is a complete draftable strategy, not just a pair of colors. A complete archetype definition has:

**Name.** Flavorful. Sets a tone the drafter can remember. "Skybound Scholars" is more memorable than "WU flyers."

**Strategy in one sentence.** What does a typical deck *do* to win?

**Speed.** Aggressive (wants to end games by turn 6-7), midrange (6-10), or controlling (8+). The speed tells you what mana curve the deck wants and what removal it needs.

**Key mechanic(s).** Which of the set's named mechanics this archetype leans on. Every mechanic should be home to at least one archetype; most should be home to two.

**Signpost uncommons.** Two gold uncommons, one **enabler** (supports the playstyle) and one **payoff** (rewards committing to it). These two cards telegraph the archetype to drafters who don't know the set yet. If a drafter opens one signpost uncommon and passes the other, the whole table has learned something about the environment.

**Required commons.** A short list of card shapes the archetype needs to actually function: "cheap evasive bodies," "a conditional counterspell," "a card-draw outlet." These become design requirements in Phase 5.

**Enemy archetypes.** Which other archetypes prey on this one, and which it preys on. Drafters need *some* rock-paper-scissors in the format to keep it interesting.

## The 20-card rule of thumb

An archetype needs roughly **20 commons that actively support it** to be draftable at an 8-player pod. That's not 20 commons per color — it's 20 commons across both its colors that either do the archetype's thing or can be slotted into it. The reason is arithmetic: 8 drafters each take ~42-45 cards, and each archetype on average feeds ~1.6 drafters. If your archetype has 10 supporting commons, only one drafter can find it; the second drafter in that pair is left with scraps.

This is why "commons carry the set" isn't a slogan, it's a constraint. If you can't name 20 commons that support an archetype, the archetype isn't real.

## Signpost design

A signpost uncommon is a sales pitch for an archetype disguised as a card. The rules:

**The enabler** should be a card that's *fine in the deck but not thrilling* on its own. Its job is to get picked first and then attract the rest of the archetype. Usually a mid-cost creature with a relevant keyword or a cheap utility effect.

**The payoff** should be the card that makes a drafter say "ah — *this* is what this deck does." Usually more situational, but the situation it needs is one the deck is already trying to create. A WU card that draws a card whenever you attack with three or more flyers is a payoff: it tells you the deck wants lots of flyers, and it rewards you for having them.

Payoffs should not be so powerful that they *replace* the archetype — they should *describe* it. A payoff that wins the game on its own warps the archetype into "draft this one card." That's a rare effect, not a signpost uncommon.

## How mechanics map to archetypes

Map every named mechanic to 1–3 archetypes as its *home*. If Kindle is a burn-expansion mechanic in red with some white, its homes might be RW (aggro burn), UR (spell-slinger burn), and BR (sacrifice-triggered burn). Each home archetype uses Kindle differently, so the mechanic feels different in each deck, and the mechanic pulls weight in multiple places at once.

Mechanics that don't have an archetype home are decorative. Decorative mechanics are the ones players forget by the end of the prerelease. If you can't name an archetype that *needs* a mechanic, cut the mechanic or rework it until some archetype does.

## The archetype grid as a balance check

Once you have all ten archetypes defined, look at them together. Ask:

- Do all five colors appear in exactly four archetypes? (Each color is in four pairs.)
- Is there at least one aggressive archetype, one midrange, and one controlling? A format of all-aggro is boring; a format of all-control is worse.
- Does every new mechanic have at least one clear home?
- Does every archetype have a distinct identity, or are two pairs doing basically the same thing? If WU and UW are both "flyers" (they're the same pair, obviously, but think: if WU and WR are both "aggro creatures with lifelink," something is wrong), differentiate them.
- Are there rock-paper-scissors relationships? You want at least one cycle where A beats B beats C beats A, so that no single archetype dominates the metagame.

Archetypes that fail this check get revised in Phase 4, not Phase 5. It's much cheaper to change an archetype definition than to rebalance a hundred cards.
