# IP Research Wisdom Catalog

Failure stories, counterintuitive insights, evolved thinking, and named heuristics for Universes Beyond IP adaptation. Consult this when making cataloging and prioritization decisions — these lessons were extracted from every published UB product.

## Failure Stories

### 1. Spider-Man — Tone mismatch with Magic's identity

**The problem:** Spider-Man's modern New York setting, superhero genre, and brightly-colored spandex aesthetic clashed fundamentally with Magic's fantasy frame. LotR and Warhammer succeeded because they're inherently fantasy or adjacent — Spider-Man is not.
**The damage:** Fans described it as the set they were "least excited about ever." Prerelease sales dropped. Collector Booster prices plummeted. WotC's internal surveys implied it underperformed. No MTG Arena support due to licensing (Wizards had to create a separate reskin "Through the Omenpaths"), fragmenting the product.
**Root cause:** The IP's tone and setting are incompatible with Magic's core aesthetic. No amount of research or cataloging fixes a fundamental tone mismatch. The researcher must flag this risk early.
**Lesson:** Before deep-diving into an IP, assess tone compatibility first. Fantasy, science fantasy, gothic, and mythological IPs translate well. Modern/urban/superhero IPs face structural resistance.

### 2. TMNT (Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles) — Failed audience conversion

**The problem:** More than half of WPN member stores reported negative community reception. TMNT fans who bought the cards showed zero interest in learning Magic or joining the community. Sales were extremely slow, with market prices at or below distributor cost.
**The damage:** The entire UB value proposition collapsed — UB is supposed to bring new players into Magic, but TMNT fans bought product and left. The IP fans and the Magic audience had no overlap.
**Root cause:** Not all IP fanbases are convertible to Magic players. TMNT's fanbase skews younger, more casual, and has no built-in affinity for complex card games. The IP research phase should assess whether the target audience is likely to engage with Magic, not just whether they'll buy the product.
**Lesson:** The IP researcher should assess fan-base convertibility: does the IP's audience overlap with or plausibly extend Magic's audience? IPs whose fans enjoy strategic complexity, fantasy worldbuilding, or collectible games are better fits than IPs whose appeal is purely nostalgic or visual.

### 3. Assassin's Creed — Product-audience mismatch and shallow mining

**The problem:** The set was marketed as Modern-legal but contained almost zero Modern-playable cards. It was designed like a Commander product but shipped without Commander precon decks. It promised historical figures but delivered only three. It used the unpopular Beyond Boosters format.
**The damage:** Critics asked "who is this for?" — a devastating question for any product. Players who wanted Constructed power were disappointed. Commander players had no precon entry point. IP fans didn't see enough of the historical figures they loved.
**Root cause:** Multiple compounding failures: the product format didn't match the audience, the IP was researched too shallowly (missing beloved historical figures), and the IP's cultural moment had passed. Players engaged with Assassin's Creed for parkour gameplay, not narrative or characters — making a character-heavy legendary set a mismatch.
**Lesson:** The IP researcher must assess what the audience actually loves about the IP (gameplay? characters? world? story?) and ensure the catalog emphasizes those elements. Also: flag when an IP's cultural moment has passed.

### 4. Transformers in Brothers' War — The insert-card experiment

**The problem:** Transformers cards were inserted into Brothers' War packs as bonus cards — mixing an external IP into a Magic-native set. The card art compositions were criticized for visual confusion, and Transformers fans weren't enthusiastic about learning Magic through random inserts.
**The damage:** The approach was criticized enough that WotC moved to separated UB products afterward. The mixed-in approach satisfied neither Magic purists (who didn't want Transformers in their packs) nor Transformers fans (who weren't engaged by random inserts).
**Root cause:** UB products work best as dedicated releases, not as inserts into Magic-native sets. The insert approach diluted both audiences and created visual/tonal inconsistency within packs.
**Lesson:** The IP researcher should design the catalog for a dedicated product, not an insert. The catalog's output assumes a self-contained set or Commander product.

### 5. Warhammer 40K — Color pie strain (partially mitigated)

**The problem:** Warhammer 40K naturally skews heavily toward black (death, corruption, war) and away from green (nature, growth). The grimdark setting has almost no pure white (order, peace, morality) either. Blue and black appeared in three of four Commander decks; white and green appeared in only one each.
**The damage:** The color imbalance wasn't fully fixable. Designers prioritized "visual distinctiveness between decks over strict lore-accurate color assignments" and used broader faction keywords to manage complexity. But the result was that some color assignments felt stretched.
**What went right:** Despite the strain, the product was commercially successful because the IP's tone matched Magic's dark fantasy frame, the factions mapped cleanly to deck structures, and the mechanical translations (Squad, Ravenous) were excellent. The color problem was managed, not solved.
**Lesson:** Color pie mismatch is inevitable in UB — the question is how severe it is and how transparently the researcher flags it. The catalog must compute the color distribution across all cataloged elements and highlight gaps prominently.

### 6. Doctor Who — Time counters overwhelm

**The problem:** The time-travel deck originally used time counters, age counters, AND lore counters simultaneously. Playtesting revealed this created too much processing overhead — players couldn't track three separate counter systems while also following the game.
**The damage:** The design team had to strip out two of the three counter types, losing mechanical texture that represented the IP well. The final product was simpler but lost some of the temporal-mechanics depth.
**Root cause:** IP researchers identified too many translatable systems without considering the cognitive budget. Just because an IP has many distinct systems doesn't mean they should all become mechanics.
**Lesson:** When cataloging IP systems for mechanical translation, prioritize ruthlessly. Flag the 2-3 most iconic systems, not every system the IP contains. Downstream designers can only use 2-4 new mechanics.

### 7. "Five versions of Peter Parker" — Character version dilution

**The problem:** In Spider-Man (and as a general risk for character-focused IPs), having too many versions of the same protagonist dilutes the set. Players want variety — seeing the same character repeatedly feels like padding.
**The damage:** Fan criticism of "too many versions of the main character" reduces the sense of discovery and makes the set feel smaller than it is.
**Root cause:** The IP researcher over-indexed on the protagonist's story arc (many moments = many cards) rather than distributing across the supporting cast. The must-include list was too top-heavy.
**Lesson:** Major characters should get 2-3 versions maximum. The researcher should explicitly cap protagonist versions and distribute remaining slots to the supporting cast. Use the formula: Tier 1 characters get 2-3 cards, Tier 2 get 1, Tier 3 get 1 at uncommon, Tier 4 get flavor text references.

## Counterintuitive Insights

### 1. Philosophy determines color, not abilities or aesthetics

Beginners assign colors based on a character's powers (fire magic = red) or visual aesthetic (dark costume = black). Experts assign colors based on the character's values, motivations, and methods. Gandalf the Grey is Blue-Red (cunning + impulsive) but Gandalf the White is mono-White (authority, protection). The same character shifts colors because his *philosophy* changed, not his abilities. When Doctor Who's Donna Noble was clearly mono-red by personality but the deck structure wanted mono-white, designers went mono-red — flavor wins.

### 2. The IP should have a "subject matter amateur" on the design team

Rosewater insists that the design team include someone unfamiliar with the IP. Every card must work as a compelling Magic card even for players who've never encountered the IP. If a card only makes sense with IP knowledge, it needs redesign. This means the IP researcher's catalog must include enough context that downstream designers can evaluate cards from both informed and uninformed perspectives.

### 3. Green is always the hardest color to fill

Across every UB product, green (nature, growth, tradition, community, instinct) is the most difficult color to populate. Spider-Man's urban setting has no green connection. Warhammer 40K's grimdark aesthetic resists green's nurturing philosophy. Even LotR, with its Ents and Hobbits, had a narrow green pool. The IP researcher must proactively identify green-compatible elements — they won't be obvious.

### 4. Fans love the IP characters they DON'T expect to see more than the ones they do

The knowledge pyramid isn't just about inclusion — it's about surprise. Players expect to see Gandalf and Frodo. They're delighted to see Bill the Pony and Rosie Cotton. The "deep cuts" at uncommon and in flavor text generate more fan goodwill per slot than the third version of the protagonist. Invest research effort in the mid-tier and deep-cut layers, not just the obvious must-includes.

### 5. Creature reprints are nearly impossible in non-fantasy IPs

Warhammer 40K managed to reprint only a single creature across all four Commander decks. The IP's aesthetic was so different from Magic's fantasy creatures that almost nothing fit. Sci-fi, modern, and superhero IPs have this problem acutely. The IP researcher should flag reprint potential early — if the IP can't support reprints, the set needs more new designs (and more design time).

### 6. What fans love about an IP isn't always what you think

Assassin's Creed fans engaged with the franchise for parkour gameplay, not narrative or characters — making a character-heavy legendary card set a mismatch. The researcher must identify what the fanbase *actually* values, which may differ from what the IP's marketing emphasizes.

## Evolved Thinking

### 1. From "include everything" to "the 5-tier rating system"

**Old approach:** Try to include every notable element from the IP, leading to shallow coverage of too many things.
**New approach (Final Fantasy model):** Create a spreadsheet with every candidate element. Rate each 1-5 using multiple data sources (internal fans, IP partner input, external surveys). Only 5s are mandatory; 4s are preferred; 1-3 are discretionary. Final Fantasy rated 600+ concepts this way, producing a decisive priority list.
**Why it changed:** The Final Fantasy set spans 16 games — without a rating system, every game's fans would demand equal representation, producing a set that satisfies nobody. The rating system forces objectivity.

### 2. From "faction-based structure" to "audience-era structure" for multi-era IPs

**Old approach:** Divide the IP by in-universe factions (heroes vs. villains, etc.).
**New approach (Doctor Who model):** Divide by when fans started engaging with the IP. Doctor Who's four Commander decks were organized by viewer era (Classic, 9th-11th Doctor, 12th-13th Doctor, Villains) because that's how fans self-identify. Assassin's Creed mapped historical eras to existing Magic plane archetypes.
**Why it changed:** Faction-based divisions fragmented the IP unnaturally. Audience-era divisions match how fans actually think about and identify with the property.

### 3. From "UB inserts in regular sets" to "dedicated UB products"

**Old approach:** Insert UB cards into Magic-native sets (Transformers in Brothers' War).
**New approach:** Dedicated, self-contained UB releases (LotR as a full set, Warhammer as Commander decks).
**Why it changed:** The insert approach satisfied neither Magic purists nor IP fans. Dedicated products let the IP breathe and find its own audience.

### 4. From "match abilities to colors" to "match philosophy to colors"

**Old approach:** Assign colors based on a character's powers (fire = red, healing = white).
**New approach:** Assign colors based on values, motivations, and methods. A character who uses fire but values freedom and passion is red for the right reason; a character who uses fire but values order and discipline might be white.
**Why it changed:** Ability-based mapping produced color assignments that felt wrong to both Magic players and IP fans. Philosophy-based mapping produces assignments that feel right even when surprising.

### 5. From "IP dictates everything" to "IP informs, Magic governs"

**Old approach:** Bend Magic's rules to perfectly represent the IP (leading to broken mechanics, color pie violations, or unfun gameplay).
**New approach:** The IP provides flavor and inspiration, but Magic's gameplay requirements are non-negotiable. If strict IP accuracy breaks gameplay, gameplay wins. Rosewater: "flavor is very important but good gameplay is even more important."
**Why it changed:** Early UB products showed that slavish IP fidelity at the expense of gameplay quality produced products that neither IP fans nor Magic players enjoyed.

## Named Heuristics and Tests

### 1. The Tone Compatibility Test

**Question:** Does the IP's genre, setting, and aesthetic coexist naturally with Magic's fantasy card game frame?
**Application:** Fantasy, science fantasy, gothic, mythological, and dark IPs translate well (LotR, Warhammer, Final Fantasy). Modern/urban, superhero, and purely comedic IPs face structural resistance (Spider-Man, TMNT). If the IP requires significant aesthetic stretching to feel like a Magic card, flag this as a high-risk factor.
**Diagnostic:** Imagine a common creature from this IP printed as a Magic card. Does it look and feel like it belongs in a booster pack alongside Innistrad and Zendikar cards? If not, the tone fit is poor.

### 2. The Knowledge Pyramid Sort

**Question:** For every element in the catalog, which tier of the knowledge pyramid does it belong to?
**Application:** Bottom tier (everyone knows) → mythic/rare, must-include. Middle tier (fans know) → rare/uncommon. Top tier (deep cuts) → uncommon/flavor text. Every element gets exactly one tier. If you can't tier an element, you don't understand the IP's audience well enough.
**Diagnostic:** Show the element's name to three people with different IP familiarity levels (casual, fan, expert). If the casual recognizes it → bottom tier. If only the fan and expert do → middle. If only the expert → top.

### 3. The Green Audit

**Question:** Which IP elements could plausibly map to green (nature, growth, tradition, community, instinct)?
**Application:** Run this audit early, because green is always underrepresented. Look for: nature-connected characters, growth/evolution themes, traditional/conservative factions, community-focused groups, animal/beast creatures, agricultural settings.
**Diagnostic:** If you can't find at least 15-20 green-compatible elements, the IP has a severe color gap that downstream designers must compensate for.

### 4. The Must-Include / Riot Test

**Question:** If this element were missing from the final set, would the IP's fanbase notice and complain?
**Application:** Apply to every element in the catalog. Only the elements that pass this test go on the must-include list. Most elements fail — fans tolerate far more omissions than designers expect. The must-include list should be 30-50 elements, not 200.
**Diagnostic:** Search fan forums for "I hope they include X" posts. The characters/moments/items mentioned most frequently are your must-includes.

### 5. The Version Cap Rule

**Question:** How many card versions does each character need?
**Application:** Tier 1 characters (protagonist, main villain): 2-3 versions, each at a different story moment and in different colors. Tier 2 (major supporting cast): 1 version. Tier 3 (minor but recognized): 1 version at uncommon. Tier 4 (deep cuts): flavor text, spell art, or card name references only.
**Diagnostic:** If any single character has more than 3 cards, you've over-indexed on them at the expense of cast breadth.

### 6. The System Translation Inventory

**Question:** What interactive systems does the IP have that could become Magic mechanics?
**Application:** List every distinctive system the IP uses (combat systems, magic systems, resource systems, relationship systems, progression systems). For each, note a candidate MTG translation. Flag the 2-3 most iconic systems for priority development. Don't catalog more than 5 — downstream designers have a limited complexity budget.
**Diagnostic:** A "system" is an interactive process with rules, not a static concept. "The Force" is a concept; "lightsaber combat styles" is a system. "Radiation" is a concept; "accumulating radiation damage over time" is a system.

### 7. The Creature Grid Check

**Question:** Does the IP provide creatures at all sizes, across all colors, with the evasion and combat keywords Magic needs?
**Application:** Magic requires small creatures (1-2 mana), medium creatures (3-4 mana), and large creatures (5+ mana) in every color. It needs flyers, it needs ground creatures, it needs tokens. If the IP lacks any of these categories, flag the gap.
**Diagnostic:** Fill out a 5x3 grid (5 colors × 3 sizes). For each cell, name an IP creature that fits. Empty cells are creature grid gaps that downstream designers must solve.

### 8. The Fan Love Diagnostic

**Question:** What does the IP's fanbase actually love about this IP?
**Application:** Is it the characters? The world? The gameplay? The story? The aesthetic? The humor? Different IPs are loved for different reasons, and the catalog must emphasize the beloved aspect. LotR is loved for characters and language → character-heavy set with Tolkien quotes. Fallout is loved for exploration and dark humor → system-translation mechanics and irreverent flavor text.
**Diagnostic:** Search "why I love [IP]" on fan communities. The most common answers should drive catalog priority.
